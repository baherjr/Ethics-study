Per the lecture: air is 99.9% nitrogen, oxygen, water vapor, and inert gases. Human activities release substances that disrupt this balance and harm humans, plants, and animals.

🏙️Cairo case

Cairo — Black Clouds

The "black clouds" phenomenon in Cairo is caused by emissions from annual burning of rice stubble in rural areas, combined with car exhaust fumes.

03 / OUTDOOR AIR POLLUTION

Four major causes

🌆Cause 1

Smog

Caused by chemical reactions between pollutants from different sources — mainly automobile exhaust and industrial emissions. Cities like Cairo are hotspots, especially in warm months.

  • Input emissions: NOₓ · VOC · SO₂ · PM
  • Produces ozone + particles + greenhouse gases
🌧️Cause 2

Acid Rain

Rainwater with increased acidity beyond clean levels. When a pollutant (e.g. sulfuric acid) combines with water droplets in the air, rain or snow becomes acidified.

  • Damages plants by destroying leaves
  • Poisons soil
  • Changes chemistry of lakes & streams
  • Negatively affects wildlife
🌡️Cause 3

Greenhouse Effect / Global Warming

Build-up of CO₂ in the atmosphere from burning fuels. Plants convert CO₂ back to O₂, but human CO₂ release exceeds what plants can process. Deforestation + acid rain make it worse.

  • CO₂ acts like a blanket trapping heat
  • Even a few degrees → climate shifts
  • Possibility of polar ice caps melting
☀️Cause 4

Ozone Depletion

Ozone = natural molecule containing three oxygen molecules. The ozone layer lives in the stratosphere and protects earth from harmful UV radiation.

  • CFCs from aerosol cans, cooling systems, refrigerators
  • Remove ozone → "holes" open in the layer
  • UV reaches earth → skin cancer + plant/wildlife damage
04 / GREENHOUSE MECHANISM

How warming actually works

The greenhouse effect — 4 stages
Stage 1

Solar energy from the sun passes through the atmosphere

Stage 2

Some energy reflects back out to space

Stage 3

Earth's surface is heated by the sun and radiates heat back toward space

Stage 4

Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere trap some of the heat — the warming

07 / WATER POLLUTION

How water gets polluted

🧪Cause 1

Sewage & fertilizers

Contain nutrients like nitrates and phosphates. In excess, they over-stimulate the growth of aquatic plants and algae.

  • Clog waterways
  • Use up dissolved oxygen as they decompose
  • Block light from reaching deeper waters
🌧️Cause 2

Silt & suspended solids

Silt and other suspended solids (e.g. soil) wash off:

  • Plowed fields
  • Construction & logging sites
  • Urban areas
  • Eroded river banks (when it rains)
08 / GROUNDWATER

The hidden reservoir

💧Key facts

Groundwater — the 95% fact

  • 95% of all fresh water on earth is groundwater
  • Found in natural rock formations
  • A large percentage of the population — especially in rural areas — relies on it for drinking water
  • Contamination risk: sewage leakage and waste disposal

10 / EIS

Environmental Impact Statement

📋What it is

Definition

When engineering projects are planned, potential environmental impacts exist. For federally funded projects these are especially important to the public. The impacts and their magnitude are reported in the EIS.

  • Used in environmental regulation
  • Used in litigation
⚖️Core principle

The fundamental rule

"Any use of natural resources at a rate higher than nature's capacity to restore itself can result in pollution of air, water, and land."

11 / FUNDAMENTAL DRIVERS OF POLLUTION

The three root causes

🏭1st driver

Industrialization

The 1st fundamental cause. Fossil fuels (oil, gas, coal) are the main sources of pollution.

👥2nd driver

Population Growth

The 2nd fundamental cause. Demand for food and goods rises with population → expanded production & use of natural resources → more pollution.

🌍3rd driver

Globalization

Developing countries often have looser environmental laws. Combined with population growth and cheap labor, industry moves to "pollution havens".

12 / PRIMARY vs SECONDARY CAUSES

Production and consumption

🏗️Primary

Production

The primary cause — the full cycle of extracting and processing natural resources, then selling through major sectors.

  • Manufacturing industry
  • Power generation
  • Road / rail / air transport
  • Agriculture & timber production
🛒Secondary

Consumption

The secondary cause — comes after and depends on production.

  • Individual consumers
  • Residential sector
  • Commercial sector
  • Social sector
🎯It's not just volume

Two additional factors beyond sheer volume

  • Inefficient methods of production
  • Careless disposal of post-consumption waste (bottles etc. that could be recycled)
13 / KYOTO PROTOCOL

The 1997 climate treaty

📜1997

Kyoto Protocol

Objective: stabilize greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would prevent dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system.

  • 175 Parties have ratified the Protocol
  • 36 countries + EEC required to reduce emissions below specified levels
  • Notable exceptions: United States and Australia

Environmental Impact Statement

Pollution drivers · Primary/secondary causes

Definition

Whenever engineering projects or programs are planned, there are potential impacts upon the environment. When the projects are federally funded, impacts become more important to the public. The magnitudes are reported in Environmental Impact Statements, which are important in environmental regulation and litigation.

Core principle

"Any use of natural resources at a rate higher than nature's capacity to restore itself can result in pollution of air, water, and land."

Beyond excessive volume — two deeper factors

  • Inefficient methods of production
  • Careless & thoughtless disposal of post-consumption waste (bottles etc. that could be recycled)

Fundamental Drivers of Pollution

  • 1st · Industrialization — fossil fuels (oil, gas, coal) are the main pollution source
  • 2nd · Population growth — demand for food/goods rises → expanded production → more pollution
  • 3rd · Globalization — looser laws in developing countries + cheap labor → industry moves to "pollution havens"

Primary vs Secondary Causes

  • Production = PRIMARY cause — the full cycle of extracting & processing natural resources. Sectors: manufacturing industry, power generation, road/rail/air transport, agriculture & timber production.
  • Consumption = SECONDARY cause — comes after and depends on production. Sectors: individual consumers, residential, commercial, social.

Kyoto Protocol (1997)

The international climate treaty

Objective

"Stabilization of greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would prevent dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system."

Key facts

  • Signed in 1997
  • 175 Parties have ratified the Protocol
  • 36 countries + the EEC are required to reduce greenhouse gas emissions below levels specified for each of them in the treaty
  • Notable exceptions: United States and Australia
  • EEC = European Economic Community
