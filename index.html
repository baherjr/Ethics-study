Introduction to Engineering & Ethics

Origin of "Engineer" · 4 Eras · Responsibilities · Ethics vs Morals · 4 Ethical Theories · Hammurabi

1.1 — What is an Engineer?

Latin Origin

The word "Engineer" derives from the Latin Ingeniator — meaning ingenious, to devise or construct.

US NAE definition: someone who understands scientific methods and engineering under constraint, applied in a technological society.
5 Engineer Responsibilities
  1. Safety of the public
  2. Welfare of society
  3. Environmental concerns
  4. Abide by laws, regulations, ethical codes
  5. Produce adequate, cost-efficient, durable products

1.2 — 4 Eras of Engineering History

1
Pre-Scientific RevolutionAncient — Renaissance. Trial and error. Egyptian Pyramids. Leonardo da Vinci.
2
Industrial Revolution18th–early 19th century. Steam engines replaced muscle power. Engineers became scientific professionals.
3
Second Industrial RevolutionPre-WWII. Electricity, cars, airplanes, telecommunications, mass production. NOT steam engines!
4
Information RevolutionLate 20th century — present. Microelectronics + computers handle intellectual tasks.
⚠ MCQ Trap: The 2nd Industrial Revolution = electricity + mass production. Steam engines = 1st Industrial Revolution.

1.3 — Ethics vs Morals

ConceptDefinitionSource
EthicsRules from external sourcesWorkplace codes, religious principles
MoralsIndividual's own internal principlesPersonal conscience and values
Ethics = EXTERNAL. Morals = INTERNAL. Sources of ethics: Religion · Custom · Conscience

1.4 — 4 Ethical Theories

Utilitarianism

Results over rules. Greatest good for the greatest number. Originally a guide for legislators.

Deontology

Duty first. Act rationally; give moral weight to equality of all humans. Resist temptation if duty says no.

Social Justice

Distributive justice. Rights and duties exist between people AND governments AND individuals.

Virtue Theory

Character over rules or results. Emphasizes virtuous qualities. Aristotle is its first advocate.

Aristotle → Virtue Theory. Utilitarianism → results. Deontology → duty. Social Justice → distributive justice.

1.5 — Legal Framework

Code of Hammurabi

The first structural code in history — the earliest known structural design code. Codes and standards set minimum requirements to ensure public safety. Ethics codes address conflicts of interest.

EasyEthical Theories
Which philosopher first advocated Virtue Theory? Briefly describe what it emphasizes.

Aristotle is recognized as the first philosopher to advocate Virtue Theory. It emphasizes virtuous character qualities over formal rules or useful results — the question isn't "what should I do?" but "what kind of person should I be?"

MediumMCQ Trap
A student claims the Second Industrial Revolution is symbolized by steam engines. Are they correct? Explain.
▾

Incorrect. Steam engines define the First Industrial Revolution (18th–early 19th century). The Second Industrial Revolution (pre-WWII) is characterized by electricity, cars, airplanes, telecommunications, and mass production. This distinction appears repeatedly in MCQs.

MediumEthical Theories
Compare Utilitarianism and Deontology. What does each prioritize and how do they differ on decision-making?
▾

Utilitarianism focuses on results — the greatest good for the greatest number. Originally a guide for legislators. The morality of an act is judged by its consequences.

Deontology focuses on duty — act rationally, give moral weight to the equality of all humans, and resist temptation even when consequences seem favorable. The morality of an act is judged by the nature of the act itself, not its outcome.

MediumSocial Justice
What is the core principle of Social Justice theory? How does it differ from Utilitarianism?
▾

Social Justice is based on distributive justice — the fair distribution of goods and resources in society. Rights and duties exist not only between people and governments, but also between individuals.

Difference from Utilitarianism: Utilitarianism could justify harming a minority to benefit the majority. Social Justice explicitly protects individual rights regardless of the majority outcome.

HardScenario
An engineer discovers their company's product has a flaw that affects 1% of users but fixing it will bankrupt the company and harm 2000 employees. Apply each of the 4 ethical theories to this dilemma.
▾

Utilitarianism: Might allow hiding the flaw — if harm to 1% of users is outweighed by saving 2000 jobs. Focuses on the net outcome for all.

Deontology: Must disclose — duty to truth and safety overrides financial consequences. The act of concealment is inherently wrong regardless of consequences.

Social Justice: Users' right to safety cannot be traded away for economic benefit. Must fix and disclose, find another way to handle bankruptcy.

Virtue Theory: An engineer of good character would disclose — honesty and integrity define who they are, independent of consequences.

2.1 — Professional Ethics

Definition

Principles that govern the behavior of a person or group in a business environment.

9 Components Count matters

Honesty · Integrity · Transparency · Accountability · Confidentiality · Objectivity · Respect · Obedience to the law · Loyalty

4 Aims
  1. Understand moral values guiding engineering
  2. Resolve moral issues in the profession
  3. Justify moral judgments
  4. Assure clients of high standards

2.2 — ASME Code of Ethics

4 Fundamental Principles
  1. Use knowledge for human welfare
  2. Be honest and impartial; serve with fidelity
  3. Increase competence and prestige of profession
  4. Support professional and technical societies
7 Fundamental Canons
  1. Hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public
  2. Perform services only in areas of competence
  3. Issue public statements only objectively and truthfully
  4. Act as faithful agents; avoid conflicts of interest
  5. Build reputation on merit; don't compete unfairly
  6. Uphold honor, integrity, dignity of the profession
  7. Continue professional development; support juniors
Canon 1 = ALWAYS hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the PUBLIC. This is the most-tested canon.

MediumCase Study
Hyatt Regency (1981): describe what happened, the death toll, and the root ethical failure.
▾

On July 17, 1981, the 4th and 2nd floor walkways of the Hyatt Regency hotel in Kansas City collapsed during a dance competition attended by 1,600 people. 114 people died.

Root cause: The contractor altered the original engineering firm's design — but the modification made the walkways weaker, not stronger. The engineering firm allegedly approved the change without performing basic structural calculations.

MediumCase Study
Challenger (1986): What was the flaw, when was it known, and what was the ethical failure?
▾

The O-ring seal in the solid rocket booster failed on January 28, 1986, allowing hot gas to reach the external fuel tank. All 7 crew members perished.

The flaw was known to NASA managers since 1977 — 9 years before the disaster. The ethical failure: engineers explicitly warned against launching in cold conditions the night before. NASA management overrode their objections and approved the launch.

MediumPlagiarism
A student submits the same report to two different courses. Is this plagiarism? Which form?
▾

Yes — this is Multiple Submission, one of the 10 recognized forms of plagiarism. Submitting one paper for credit in two different classes is academic misconduct even though the work is originally your own, because each course expects original work done specifically for it.

HardUnethical Comms
An engineer knows a drug interaction risk but omits it from instructions because it would complicate the product manual. Which of the 5 unethical communication concerns applies, and what should they have done?
▾

This is Omitting Safety Warnings (concern #5) AND potentially Withholding Adverse Information (concern #3).

The engineer should have included the drug interaction warning clearly in the manual — ASME Canon 1 requires holding public safety paramount. The fact that it complicates the document is irrelevant. This type of omission can lead to patient harm and professional liability.

HardDilemma Framework
Apply the 4-step dilemma framework to the Challenger scenario. At what step did NASA management fail?
▾

Step 1: Analyze carefully — engineers had analyzed and identified the O-ring risk in cold temperatures. This step was completed correctly by the engineers.

Step 2: Consider multiple angles — management considered only schedule and economic pressures, not the engineering safety data. Failed here.

Step 3: Consult with others — engineers were consulted but overruled. The consultation happened but the feedback was ignored. Failed here.

Step 4: Think long and hard — management rushed the decision under pressure. Failed catastrophically here.

A worker is found unconscious at a construction site with a visible bone fracture and bleeding. Apply the First Aid priorities correctly.

Critical Thinking & Reasoning

10 Standards · 8 Elements · 8 Traits · Deduction vs Induction · Worked Examples

5.1 — Critical Thinking Definition

Critical thinking is routinely applying intellectual standards to the elements of thought as they seek to develop the traits of a mature engineering mind.

5.2 — 10 Intellectual Standards

  1. Clarity — understandable; meaning can be grasped
  2. Accuracy — free from errors or distortions; true
  3. Relevance — relating to the matter at hand
  4. Logicalness — parts make sense together; no contradictions
  5. Breadth — encompassing multiple viewpoints (wide)
  1. Precision — exact to the necessary level of detail
  2. Significance — focusing on the important, not trivial
  3. Completeness — something is whole; nothing is missing
  4. Fairness — justifiable; non-self-serving; not one-sided
  5. Depth — containing complexities and multiple viewpoints (deep)
Breadth = encompassing multiple viewpoints (wide/broad). Depth = complexities (deep). Don't mix them up!

5.3 — 8 Elements of Thought

  • Purposes — whenever we think, we think for a purpose
  • Questions — to answer a question or solve a problem
  • Points of view — within a point of view
  • Assumptions — based on assumptions
  • Implications — leading to implications and consequences
  • Information — we use data, facts, and experiences
  • Inferences — to make inferences and judgments
  • Concepts — based on concepts and theories

5.4 — 8 Intellectual Traits

Humility

Knowledge of ignorance — sensitive to what you know and what you DON'T know.

Autonomy

Thinking for oneself while adhering to standards of rationality.

Integrity

Holding yourself to the same standards you expect from others — NO DOUBLE STANDARDS.

Courage

The disposition to question beliefs about which you feel strongly.

Perseverance

Working through intellectual complexities despite frustrations inherent in the task.

Confidence in Reason

Belief that human interests are best served by giving freest play to reason.

Empathy

Awareness of the need to actively entertain views that differ from your own — especially those you strongly disagree with.

Fairmindedness

Treating all viewpoints alike without reference to one's own feelings or vested interests.

5.5 — Deduction vs. Induction

Deductive Reasoning

Reasoning from general premises to specific, certain conclusions. "Formal logic."

Conclusions are: certain · inevitable · inescapable
Major premise: All tortoises are vegetarians Minor premise: Bessie is a tortoise Conclusion: Therefore Bessie IS a vegetarian
Inductive Reasoning

Reasoning from specific cases to general, uncertain conclusions. “Informal / everyday logic.”

Conclusions are: probable · reasonable · plausible · believable
Observation: Biff has an anchor tattoo on his arm Conclusion: He PROBABLY served in the Navy
Neither form is preferred over the other — both are equally compelling and persuasive (Hollihan & Baske, 1994).

Law of the Sea: vessels beyond 12 miles = international waters, cannot be boarded → US Coast Guard violates this lawDeductiveKnown legal premises → certain specific conclusion

MediumStandards Trap
What is the difference between "Breadth" and "Depth" as intellectual standards? Students frequently confuse them.
▾

Breadth: Encompassing multiple viewpoints — thinking widely across different perspectives. Wide coverage. Example: considering an engineering decision from the perspectives of the client, the public, regulators, and workers.

Depth: Containing complexities — thinking deeply into the nuances of a single question. Thorough exploration. Example: fully understanding all the physical mechanisms behind a structural failure rather than just knowing it collapsed.

Memory aid: Breadth = WIDE. Depth = DEEP.

MediumReasoning
Classify each: (a) "All engineers must be licensed → Ahmed is an engineer → Ahmed must be licensed." (b) "Every bridge I've inspected this year had corrosion → bridges in this region probably have a corrosion problem."
▾

(a) Deductive reasoning. Starts with a known general rule (all engineers must be licensed) and applies it to a specific case (Ahmed) to reach a certain conclusion. Classic syllogism structure.

(b) Inductive reasoning. Starts with specific observations (each individual bridge) and draws a general but uncertain conclusion about all bridges in the region. The conclusion is probable but could be wrong — there may be bridges without corrosion that weren't inspected.

HardElements of Thought
Apply the 8 elements of thought to the following engineering decision: "Should we proceed with construction despite the subsoil report suggesting potential instability?"
▾

Purpose: Make a safe, cost-effective decision about whether to proceed with construction.
Question: Is the subsoil sufficiently stable to proceed, and what are the risks if we don't investigate further?
Point of view: Engineering safety perspective, client's financial perspective, public safety perspective.
Assumptions: That the subsoil report is accurate; that known engineering standards apply; that budget constraints are fixed.
Information: The subsoil report, site geology data, structural load calculations, similar case precedents.
Inferences: If instability is confirmed, foundation design must change; if ignored, collapse risk increases.
Concepts: Geotechnical engineering, factor of safety, building codes, negligence.
Implications: Proceeding without investigation could lead to structural failure, liability under Article 651, and loss of life.

HardRoofline Example
Discuss the power generation table: Companies 1 (3450 MW gen, 3000 MW load, $100/MWh), 2 (1488 MW, 1200 MW, $50/MWh), 3 (6220 MW, 6900 MW, $25/MWh). Which company needs extra power and what is the cheapest strategy?
▾

Reserve Power = Generated Capacity − Load Supplied:
Company 1: 3450 − 3000 = +450 MW reserve
Company 2: 1488 − 1200 = +288 MW reserve
Company 3: 6220 − 6900 = −680 MW DEFICIT

Company 3 needs 680 MW of extra power.

Cheapest strategy (use inductive reasoning on costs):
Company 2 is cheaper ($50/MWh vs $100/MWh for Company 1).
Buy all 288 MW from Company 2 first → 680 − 288 = 392 MW still needed.
Buy remaining 392 MW from Company 1 at $100/MWh.

Additional observations: Company 3 has the largest average unit capacity (6220÷18 = 345.6 MW/unit). Company 2 has the smallest (1488÷17 = 87.5 MW/unit).

Construction Contract Law

Egyptian Civil Code · Arts. 647–667 · Supply · Defects · 10-Year Guarantee · Payment · Subcontracting · Termination

5.1 — What is a Construction Contract?

One party undertakes to do the work for pay; the other undertakes to pay. Its importance comes from high financial value, long duration, and external influences — consulting engineers, subcontractors, the site itself.

Three Roles
  • Contractor — executes the work; may bring material, employer's material, or both
  • Employer — pays the wage, enables the work, receives the product
  • Subcontractor — does part of the work on behalf of the contractor (who stays liable)
Contractor's 3 Core Duties
  1. Perform the work in the agreed manner
  2. Deliver on time
  3. Guarantee the work for 10 years
Employer's 3 Core Duties
  1. Enable the contractor to finish
  2. Receive the completed work
  3. Pay the wage

5.2 — Who Brings the Material? (Arts. 647–649)

Art. 647
Three Arrangements
  • Contractor brings only work — employer supplies all material
  • Contractor brings work + material together
  • Either is legal — the contract defines it
Art. 648
Contractor Brings Material

Responsible for quality and must guarantee it to the employer. Even partial supply = full responsibility for what was supplied.

Art. 649
Employer Brings Material
  • Contractor must care for it and keep records
  • Return what is left over after the job
  • Negligence or incompetence → contractor pays the wasted value

5.3 — Defective Work (Art. 650)

Art. 650(a)
Impossible to Fix

Contract is terminated without warning. No second chance, no notice required.

Art. 650(b)
Can Be Fixed
  • Contractor given a chance to repair
  • Or contract can be rescinded
  • Or another contractor repairs it at the original's expense

5.4 — The Ten-Year Guarantee (Arts. 651, 653, 654)

Art. 651
Joint Liability · 10 Years
  • Engineer + contractor are jointly liable
  • Clock starts at date of delivery
  • Covers: full collapse, partial collapse, defects that harm the facility
Art. 653
Can't Contract Out

Any clause that exempts the engineer or contractor from this liability is null and void. Not negotiable.

Art. 654
3-Year Window to Sue

Guarantee claims expire 3 years after demolition. Must act within the window.

⚠ MCQ Trap: The 10-year guarantee period (Art. 651) is NOT the same as the 3-year limitation to file suit (Art. 654). Both numbers appear in questions.

5.5 — Delivery & Payment (Arts. 655, 657–660)

Art. 655
Employer Refuses Delivery?

If there is no legitimate technical reason and a formal warning was sent, delivery is deemed complete.

Art. 659
Wage Not Specified?

Determined by value of work + contractor's expenses.

Art. 657
Unit-Price Contract
  • Extra work needed → contractor must notify (variation order)
  • No notice = forfeits the right to claim
  • If increase is huge → employer can stop work and pay only for what's done
Art. 658
Lump-Sum Contract
  • Design changes → no extra pay unless employer agrees or caused it
  • Price inflation → no compensation...
  • ...except for war or natural disasters
Art. 660
Engineer's Wage
  • Paid for design + supervision
  • Unspecified → determined by custom
  • Design not used → paid for time spent drafting it

5.6 — Subcontracting (Arts. 661–662)

Art. 661
Can a Contractor Subcontract?
  • Yes by default — and stays responsible
  • No if the contract forbids it
  • No if work requires the original's specific personal competence
Art. 662
Subcontractor's Wages
  • Can claim directly from employer
  • Capped at what employer still owes the main contractor
  • In seizure: sub's workers get priority over main contractor

5.7 — Termination (Arts. 663–667)

Art. 663
Employer Stops Work (No Reason)
  • Pays the expenses
  • Pays compensation for lost earnings
  • Court may reduce the compensation
Art. 664
Work Becomes Impossible

The contract expires automatically. No fault, no compensation — just over.

Art. 665
Sudden Accident
  • No fault → whoever supplied the material bears its loss; contractor gets no pay
  • Contractor's fault → compensates employer for supplied material
  • Employer's fault → bears material loss + compensates contractor
Art. 666
Contractor Dies
  • Personal qualifications mattered → contract expires
  • Qualifications didn't matter → contract continues with heirs
Art. 667
Settlement After Death
  • Employer pays heirs for work executed
  • Can request drawings & prepared materials — with compensation
  • Same rules if contractor can't continue for reasons beyond their control

EasyArt. 649
The employer supplies all the concrete for a project. The contractor wastes a large amount through negligence. What is the contractor's liability?
▾

The contractor must pay the value of the wasted material to the employer. Art. 649 requires the contractor to safeguard employer-supplied material, keep records of use, and return leftovers. Negligence or lack of technical competence = liability for the wasted value.

EasyArt. 650
A contractor performs work so defectively that it cannot be repaired at all. What happens?
▾

The contract is terminated without warning. Art. 650(a) — when repair is impossible, termination is automatic and immediate. No notice period is required.

EasyArt. 651
For how long after delivery are the engineer and contractor jointly liable for structural problems?
▾

10 years from the date of delivery. Art. 651 — the decennial liability covers full collapse, partial collapse, and defects that harm the facility. Both engineer and contractor are jointly (solidarily) liable.

EasyArt. 653
A construction contract includes a clause: "The engineer shall bear no liability for collapse." Is this clause enforceable?
▾

No — it is null and void. Art. 653 makes any clause that exempts the engineer or contractor from the Art. 651 decennial liability completely unenforceable, regardless of the parties' consent.

MediumArt. 654
A building partially collapses due to a defect. The demolition happens in Year 8 after delivery. By when must the employer file a guarantee claim?
▾

Within 3 years of the demolition — i.e., before Year 11. Art. 654 sets a 3-year limitation period that runs from the demolition/damage event. Do not confuse this with the 10-year liability window in Art. 651.

MediumArt. 655
An employer refuses to receive completed work and gives no technical reason, even after a formal warning is sent. What is the legal effect?
▾

The work is deemed to have been delivered. Art. 655 — refusal without a legitimate and technically acceptable reason, following an official warning, triggers deemed delivery. The employer cannot indefinitely delay acceptance.

MediumArt. 657
In a unit-price contract, the contractor realizes the work will substantially exceed the estimate but stays silent until the end. What happens to their extra-cost claim?
▾

The right to claim the increase is forfeited. Art. 657 — under a unit-price contract, the contractor must issue a variation order as soon as extra work is foreseen. Silence = forfeiture. No notice means no claim.

MediumArt. 658
A lump-sum contract runs into a major war that triples steel prices. Can the contractor claim extra compensation?
▾

Yes — war is an external event that triggers compensation. Art. 658 — lump-sum contracts exclude general price-rise claims, but external events such as war or natural disasters are exceptions where the contractor may be compensated.

HardArt. 661
Can a contractor hire a subcontractor without the employer's prior approval? When is subcontracting prohibited?
▾

Yes — subcontracting is allowed by default. The contractor remains fully responsible for the subcontractor. Exceptions under Art. 661: (1) the contract explicitly forbids it, or (2) the work requires the original contractor's specific personal competence.

HardArt. 665
During construction, a sudden fire destroys the site. The employer had supplied the materials, and no one was at fault. Who bears the material loss? Does the contractor get paid?
▾

The employer bears the material loss (they supplied it). The contractor gets no pay for work not yet completed. Art. 665 — in a no-fault sudden accident, material loss falls on whoever supplied the material, and the contractor earns nothing for the unfinished portion.

Tenders & Auctions Law

Government Contracting · 6 Methods · Insurances · Delay Fines · Termination

6.1 — Why a Special Law?

When the government is a party to a contract, extra controls apply beyond ordinary construction contract law — transparency, equal opportunity, and strict procedures for selecting contractors, protecting public money, and handling delays.

Tender (المناقصة)

The project owner seeks the lowest price among technically acceptable bids, submitted in closed envelopes.

Practice (الممارسة)

Similar to a tender, but allows applicants to compete live in-session to reduce the price further.

Bid (العطاء)

The contractor's offer — two closed envelopes: one technical, one financial. Financial envelopes are only opened for bids that were technically accepted first.

Critical Rule — Art. 1

A public tender can never be converted into a practice (public or limited). Default = public tender or public practice.

⚠ MCQ Trap: practice → tender is NOT mentioned; only tender → practice is forbidden.

6.2 — The Four Principles of Public Methods (Art. 2)

  1. Publicity — announced in daily newspapers and wide-reach media
  2. Equal opportunity — all qualified parties may participate
  3. Equality — all bidders treated the same
  4. Freedom of competition — no artificial barriers

Scope: internal (announced in Egypt only) or external (announced inside + outside Egypt).

6.3 — Six Contracting Methods (Arts. 1–8)

Default = public tender or public practice. All other methods are exceptions requiring a reasoned decision from the competent authority.

Art. 1–2
Public Tender / Public Practice ✦ Default

Subject to the 4 principles. Tender = sealed lowest price. Practice = live price competition.

Art. 3
Limited Tender

Restricts participation to specific qualified parties who meet technical competence + financial competence + good reputation.

Art. 4
Local Tender

Contract value ≤ 200,000 EGP. Limited to suppliers/contractors operating within the execution governorate.

Art. 5
Limited Practice
  • Items from a unique source only
  • Must buy from production site (e.g. extend same device)
  • Work requires specific specialists
  • National security — confidential contracting
Art. 7
Direct Agreement

Urgent cases that can't wait. Approved by the relevant official within their threshold (see table below).

Art. 8
Defense & Military Production

May use any exception method in cases of necessity. Armament contracts exempt under Law 204/1957.

6.4 — Direct Agreement Thresholds (Art. 7)

Authority Limits (EGP)
Official Supplies / Services / Consulting / Transport Business
Head of Authority ≤ 50,000 ≤ 100,000
Competent Minister / Governor ≤ 100,000 ≤ 300,000
Prime Minister (extreme necessity) No upper limit — above Minister's thresholds

6.5 — Bid Submission & Committees (Arts. 10–11)

Art. 10
Two-Envelope System
  • Envelope 1: technical offer
  • Envelope 2: financial offer
  • Financial only opened after technical acceptance
Art. 11
Committees
  • Normally: one committee opens, another decides
  • Value ≤ 50,000 EGP → single committee does both

6.6 — Cancellation (Art. 15)

Mandatory Cancellation
  • Tender is no longer needed
  • Public interest requires it
Discretionary Cancellation
  • Only one bid submitted (or one left after exclusions)
  • All/most bids come with reservations
  • Lowest bid exceeds estimated project value

6.7 — Primary & Final Insurance (Arts. 17–19)

Art. 17
Primary Insurance
  • Paid with the bid
  • Cap: 2% of estimated value
  • Missing full amount → bid excluded
  • Returned to losers automatically — no request needed
Art. 18
Final Insurance
  • Winner tops up primary → 5% total of contract value
  • 10 days from notification (local contractor)
  • 20 days from notification (foreign contractor)
  • Excess of primary refunded immediately — no request
Art. 19
Final Insurance Skipped

If the winner delivers everything and the authority accepts within the final-insurance payment window — no final insurance needed.

⚠ Trap: 2% = primary insurance cap. 5% = final insurance total. 10 days = local. 20 days = foreign.

6.8 — Delay Fines (Art. 23)

Art. 23
Automatic Fine — No Warning Needed
  • Fine kicks in automatically the moment delay occurs — no notice required
  • Max 3% of contract value: supplies, services, consulting, technical works
  • Max 10% of contract value: business contracts, transport
  • Authority may grant an extension in public interest (but fine still applies)
  • Exempt if delay caused by factors beyond contractor's control

6.9 — Termination (Arts. 24–26)

Art. 24
Automatic Termination
  • Contractor attempted bribery (directly or indirectly)
  • Contract obtained via bribery
  • Contractor went bankrupt / insolvent
  • Bribery → also removed from register + public notice
Art. 25
Breach — Two Options

If the contractor breaches any condition, the authority may either terminate the contract OR execute it at the contractor's expense via another party.

Art. 26
Consequences of Termination
  • Final insurance forfeited to the authority
  • Fines + losses deducted from any money owed to contractor
  • Insufficient? → pulled from contractor's dues at any other administrative authority

What is the key feature that distinguishes a 'practice' from a 'tender'?

A practice allows bidders to compete live in-session to reduce the price further. A tender uses only sealed envelopes; once submitted, bids are fixed. The practice adds a real-time price competition round on top of the sealed submission.

The local tender method is restricted to contracts whose value does not exceed…

EasyArt. 17
The primary insurance is capped at what percentage of the estimated project value, and what happens if it's missing from a bid?
▾

Cap is 2% of the estimated value. A bid submitted without the full primary insurance amount is automatically excluded (Art. 17). No exceptions.

MediumArt. 18
A local contractor wins a tender. They already paid 2% primary insurance. What must they do next, and by when?
▾

They must top up the primary insurance to reach 5% of the contract value (the final insurance) within 10 days of receiving the acceptance notification. Foreign contractors get 20 days. Any excess over the 5% target is refunded immediately — no request needed.

MediumArt. 7
In an urgent case, a ministry wants to contract directly for business works worth 250,000 EGP. Who has the authority to approve this?
▾

The competent minister (or governor). Art. 7 thresholds for business contracting: Head of Authority ≤ 100,000 EGP; Minister/Governor ≤ 300,000 EGP; Prime Minister above that. At 250,000 EGP, it falls within the Minister's limit.

MediumArt. 11
A tender is for a contract worth 45,000 EGP. How many committees are needed to open and decide the bid?
▾

One committee handles both opening the envelopes and deciding. Art. 11 — for bids with a value ≤ 50,000 EGP, the standard two-committee system collapses to a single committee doing both functions.

MediumArt. 15
Which of these is a MANDATORY ground for cancellation (not discretionary)? (a) Only one bid submitted. (b) Public interest requires cancellation. (c) Lowest bid exceeds estimated value.
▾

(b) — Public interest requires cancellation is mandatory. Art. 15 — mandatory grounds: the tender is no longer needed, OR public interest requires it. The other two (single bid, exceeds estimate) are discretionary grounds where the authority may cancel.

HardArt. 23
A contractor is one day late on a consulting services contract. Does the authority need to send a formal warning before the delay fine applies? What is the maximum fine?
▾

No warning is needed — the fine applies automatically the moment delay occurs (Art. 23). The maximum fine for consulting services is 3% of the contract value. The 10% cap applies only to business and transport contracts.

HardArts. 24–26
A contractor is proven to have bribed a government employee to win the bid. List all consequences beyond simple contract termination.
▾

Art. 24(a) + Art. 26 — the contractor: (1) is automatically terminated; (2) is struck from the suppliers/contractors register; (3) the removal is publicly announced by the General Authority for Government Services; (4) the final insurance is forfeited to the authority; (5) fines and losses are deducted from any money owed to the contractor, including at other administrative authorities.

Types of Construction Contracts

Lump Sum · Unit Price · Cost Plus · BOT · FIDIC — Risk allocation dressed as a pricing model

7.1 — The Four Contract Types

Lump Sum
Fixed total · Contractor bears risk

Total price agreed before work starts. Employer provides approximate drawings & specs. Contractor absorbs any quantity increase with no extra pay. Links to Civil Code Art. 658.

Unit Price
Per-unit pricing · The 25% rule

Bill of Quantities (BOQ) with a price per unit. Quantity changes handled by the 25% rule — small increases at BOQ price, large ones trigger a variation order. Links to Art. 657.

Cost Plus
Actual costs + profit margin · Employer bears risk

Contractor paid actual costs + profit margin. Three variants for how the profit margin is structured (see §7.3).

BOT
Build · Operate · Transfer · PPP model

Private company finances & builds, operates to recoup costs + profit, then transfers ownership to government. Used when no public funding is available.

7.2 — Side-by-Side Comparison

7.3 — Cost Plus: Three Profit Variants

Variant 1
Percentage of costs

Profit = a fixed percentage of actual costs. If costs rise, profit rises proportionally — can incentivize overspending.

Variant 2
Fixed profit amount

Profit = a fixed lump sum, regardless of how costs move. Contractor's margin is decoupled from spending.

Variant 3
Percentage + savings share ✦ Best alignment

Base margin = percentage of costs, plus a share of any savings below budget. This variant aligns incentives — the contractor is rewarded for efficiency.

7.4 — Unit Price: The 25% Rule

Increase < 25%
Submit at BOQ price — no renegotiation

Small overruns stay at the BOQ rate. Contractor's risk to absorb.

Increase > 25%
Variation order + new price

Large overruns trigger a variation order. Contractor sets the new price they want. Employer's risk zone.

⚠ MCQ Trap: Connect the 25% rule to Art. 657 (unit-price contracts). 25% = the exact threshold word in the lecture.

7.5 — BOT: Build · Operate · Transfer

1 · Build

A private company designs, finances, and executes the project — typically infrastructure.

2 · Operate

The company runs it long enough to recoup costs + profit from operating revenues.

3 · Transfer

Ownership handed to the government. Used when there are no financial appropriations for the project. Arabic name adds Design: DBOT — التصميم والبناء والتشغيل ونقل الملكية.

Fédération Internationale Des Ingénieurs-Conseils

An international organization speaking on behalf of consulting engineers. Publishes standard contract templates covering all requirements among employer, contractor, and engineer. Templates are named by the colour of their cover.

In Egypt: FIDIC is used only in joint projects with foreign parties when they request it. It is not the default.

Deep Dive · Contract Types

Select a type to explore in detail

Lump Sum Contract

Fixed total · Contractor bears quantity risk
Core rule

The total amount to be paid to the contractor is agreed before starting work. The employer provides drawings and specifications, but these contain approximate numbers.

Risk allocation

The contractor is responsible for any increase in the numbers without a corresponding increase in the agreed amount. This makes lump sum risky for the contractor and cost-predictable for the employer.

Key points
  • Price fixed before work starts — no surprises for the employer
  • Employer supplies approximate drawings & specs (not exact)
  • Contractor absorbs quantity increases — no extra pay
  • Links to Civil Code Art. 658 (lump-sum rules from L5)

Unit Price Contract

Per-unit pricing · The 25% rule
Core rule

There is a bill of quantities (BOQ). The contractor sets a price for each unit, and payment = unit price × actual quantity.

Handling increases — the 25% threshold
  • Increase < 25% → submit at the BOQ price (no renegotiation)
  • Increase > 25% → issue a variation order and set the new price
Risk split

Small overruns (≤ 25%) stay at BOQ rates — contractor's risk. Significant overruns (> 25%) trigger repricing — employer's risk. More balanced than lump sum. Links to Civil Code Art. 657.

Cost Plus Contract

Reimburse costs + profit margin · Three variants
Core rule

The contractor is paid for actual costs incurred + a profit margin. The lecture categorises cost-plus by how the profit margin is structured:

Three profit variants
  • Variant 1 — a percentage of costs as profit. More spending = more profit (can incentivize overspending)
  • Variant 2 — a fixed profit amount, regardless of costs (margin decoupled from spending)
  • Variant 3 — percentage of costs + share of savings below budget (best incentive alignment)
Risk

Risk lies heavily with the employer — all costs are reimbursed. Variant 3 is the one that incentivizes the contractor to control costs.

BOT · Build · Operate · Transfer

Private financing + operation → government ownership
Core rule

A private company executes the project and operates it for a set period, using revenues to recoup spending plus profit. At the end, ownership transfers to the government.

When it's used
  • Typically for infrastructure projects
  • Used when there are no financial appropriations — government lacks budget
Arabic name adds a fourth letter

DBOT — Design, Build, Operate, Transfer (التصميم والبناء والتشغيل ونقل الملكية). The company designs and finances from scratch.

FIDIC · Standard Contract Templates

Fédération Internationale Des Ingénieurs-Conseils
What it is

An international organization that speaks on behalf of consulting engineers. Publishes standard contract templates covering all requirements among employer, contractor, and engineer. Templates are identified by the colour of their cover.

The four coloured books
  • Red → Civil engineering works contracts
  • Yellow → Mechanical & electrical works contracts
  • Orange → Design & construction (turnkey) contracts
  • White → Terms between employer & consulting engineer
When it applies in Egypt

FIDIC is not the default. Used in Egypt only for joint projects with foreign parties — and only when those foreign parties request it.
