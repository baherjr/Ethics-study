1.1 — What is an Engineer?

Latin Origin

The word "Engineer" derives from the Latin Ingeniator — meaning ingenious, to devise or construct.

US NAE definition: someone who understands scientific methods and engineering under constraint, applied in a technological society.
5 Engineer Responsibilities
  1. Safety of the public
  2. Welfare of society
  3. Environmental concerns
  4. Abide by laws, regulations, ethical codes
  5. Produce adequate, cost-efficient, durable products

1.2 — 4 Eras of Engineering History

1
Pre-Scientific RevolutionAncient — Renaissance. Trial and error. Egyptian Pyramids. Leonardo da Vinci.
2
Industrial Revolution18th–early 19th century. Steam engines replaced muscle power. Engineers became scientific professionals.
3
Second Industrial RevolutionPre-WWII. Electricity, cars, airplanes, telecommunications, mass production. NOT steam engines!
4
Information RevolutionLate 20th century — present. Microelectronics + computers handle intellectual tasks.
⚠ MCQ Trap: The 2nd Industrial Revolution = electricity + mass production. Steam engines = 1st Industrial Revolution.

1.3 — Ethics vs Morals

ConceptDefinitionSource
EthicsRules from external sourcesWorkplace codes, religious principles
MoralsIndividual's own internal principlesPersonal conscience and values
Ethics = EXTERNAL. Morals = INTERNAL. Sources of ethics: Religion · Custom · Conscience

1.4 — 4 Ethical Theories

Utilitarianism

Results over rules. Greatest good for the greatest number. Originally a guide for legislators.

Deontology

Duty first. Act rationally; give moral weight to equality of all humans. Resist temptation if duty says no.

Social Justice

Distributive justice. Rights and duties exist between people AND governments AND individuals.

Virtue Theory

Character over rules or results. Emphasizes virtuous qualities. Aristotle is its first advocate.

Aristotle → Virtue Theory. Utilitarianism → results. Deontology → duty. Social Justice → distributive justice.

1.5 — Legal Framework

Code of Hammurabi

The first structural code in history — the earliest known structural design code. Codes and standards set minimum requirements to ensure public safety. Ethics codes address conflicts of interest.

EasyEthical Theories
Which philosopher first advocated Virtue Theory? Briefly describe what it emphasizes.
▾

Aristotle is recognized as the first philosopher to advocate Virtue Theory. It emphasizes virtuous character qualities over formal rules or useful results — the question isn't "what should I do?" but "what kind of person should I be?"

MediumMCQ Trap
A student claims the Second Industrial Revolution is symbolized by steam engines. Are they correct? Explain.
▾

Incorrect. Steam engines define the First Industrial Revolution (18th–early 19th century). The Second Industrial Revolution (pre-WWII) is characterized by electricity, cars, airplanes, telecommunications, and mass production. This distinction appears repeatedly in MCQs.

MediumEthical Theories
Compare Utilitarianism and Deontology. What does each prioritize and how do they differ on decision-making?
▾

Utilitarianism focuses on results — the greatest good for the greatest number. Originally a guide for legislators. The morality of an act is judged by its consequences.

Deontology focuses on duty — act rationally, give moral weight to the equality of all humans, and resist temptation even when consequences seem favorable. The morality of an act is judged by the nature of the act itself, not its outcome.

MediumSocial Justice
What is the core principle of Social Justice theory? How does it differ from Utilitarianism?
▾

Social Justice is based on distributive justice — the fair distribution of goods and resources in society. Rights and duties exist not only between people and governments, but also between individuals.

Difference from Utilitarianism: Utilitarianism could justify harming a minority to benefit the majority. Social Justice explicitly protects individual rights regardless of the majority outcome.

HardScenario
An engineer discovers their company's product has a flaw that affects 1% of users but fixing it will bankrupt the company and harm 2000 employees. Apply each of the 4 ethical theories to this dilemma.
▾

Utilitarianism: Might allow hiding the flaw — if harm to 1% of users is outweighed by saving 2000 jobs. Focuses on the net outcome for all.

Deontology: Must disclose — duty to truth and safety overrides financial consequences. The act of concealment is inherently wrong regardless of consequences.

Social Justice: Users' right to safety cannot be traded away for economic benefit. Must fix and disclose, find another way to handle bankruptcy.

Virtue Theory: An engineer of good character would disclose — honesty and integrity define who they are, independent of consequences.

Principles that govern the behavior of a person or group in a business environment.

4 Fundamental Principles
  1. Use knowledge for human welfare
  2. Be honest and impartial; serve with fidelity
  3. Increase competence and prestige of profession
  4. Support professional and technical societies
7 Fundamental Canons

MediumCase Study
Hyatt Regency (1981): describe what happened, the death toll, and the root ethical failure.
▾

On July 17, 1981, the 4th and 2nd floor walkways of the Hyatt Regency hotel in Kansas City collapsed during a dance competition attended by 1,600 people. 114 people died.

Root cause: The contractor altered the original engineering firm's design — but the modification made the walkways weaker, not stronger. The engineering firm allegedly approved the change without performing basic structural calculations.

MediumCase Study
Challenger (1986): What was the flaw, when was it known, and what was the ethical failure?
▾

The O-ring seal in the solid rocket booster failed on January 28, 1986, allowing hot gas to reach the external fuel tank. All 7 crew members perished.

The flaw was known to NASA managers since 1977 — 9 years before the disaster. The ethical failure: engineers explicitly warned against launching in cold conditions the night before. NASA management overrode their objections and approved the launch.

MediumPlagiarism
A student submits the same report to two different courses. Is this plagiarism? Which form?
▾

Yes — this is Multiple Submission, one of the 10 recognized forms of plagiarism. Submitting one paper for credit in two different classes is academic misconduct even though the work is originally your own, because each course expects original work done specifically for it.

HardUnethical Comms
An engineer knows a drug interaction risk but omits it from instructions because it would complicate the product manual. Which of the 5 unethical communication concerns applies, and what should they have done?
▾

This is Omitting Safety Warnings (concern #5) AND potentially Withholding Adverse Information (concern #3).

The engineer should have included the drug interaction warning clearly in the manual — ASME Canon 1 requires holding public safety paramount. The fact that it complicates the document is irrelevant. This type of omission can lead to patient harm and professional liability.

HardDilemma Framework
Apply the 4-step dilemma framework to the Challenger scenario. At what step did NASA management fail?
▾

Step 1: Analyze carefully — engineers had analyzed and identified the O-ring risk in cold temperatures. This step was completed correctly by the engineers.

Step 2: Consider multiple angles — management considered only schedule and economic pressures, not the engineering safety data. Failed here.

Step 3: Consult with others — engineers were consulted but overruled. The consultation happened but the feedback was ignored. Failed here.

Step 4: Think long and hard — management rushed the decision under pressure. Failed catastrophically here.

4.1 — Contract Basics

Definition

A contract between two parties: one undertakes to do something (contractor), and the other undertakes to pay (employer). Important because of: (a) high financial value, (b) long duration, (c) external influences like subcontractors and consulting engineers.

3 Contractor Obligations
  1. Perform work in the desired manner
  2. Deliver at the agreed time
  3. Guarantee work for 10 years
3 Employer Obligations
  1. Enable the contractor to complete work
  2. Receive the work from the contractor
  3. Pay the wages

4.2 — Critical Articles (MCQ Priority)

Article 651 — 10-Year Joint Guarantee ⭐

The engineer AND contractor are jointly responsible for the facility for 10 years from the date of delivery. Covers: full collapse, partial collapse, structural defects.

Starts from DELIVERY DATE — NOT from signing or construction start. This is the #1 MCQ trap on this article.
Article 653 — Exemption Clauses are Void ⭐

Any contract clause that exempts the engineer or contractor from 10-year liability is null and void. You cannot contractually escape this responsibility.

Article 654 — 3-Year Filing Deadline ⭐

Guarantee lawsuits are forfeited if 3 years pass after the demolition or defect occurs.

Pair: 10 years = guarantee PERIOD. 3 years = filing DEADLINE after demolition. Don't mix them.

EasyArticle 651
According to Article 651, when does the 10-year guarantee period begin?
▾

From the date of delivery of the completed work — NOT from the date the contract was signed, and NOT from when construction began. This is the most common MCQ trap on Article 651.

EasyArticle 654
What is the difference between the guarantee period (Article 651) and the filing deadline (Article 654)?
▾

Article 651 — Guarantee Period: 10 years from delivery date. During this time, the engineer and contractor are jointly liable for collapse and structural defects.

Article 654 — Filing Deadline: 3 years after the demolition/defect occurs. If you don't file within 3 years of the defect, your lawsuit is forfeited.

Example: A building collapses in year 8 (within the 10-year period). You have 3 more years to file the claim — until year 11 from delivery.

EasyArticle 653
A contract includes a clause stating: "The contractor is not liable for any structural defects for the first 5 years." Is this clause valid?
▾

No — null and void under Article 653. Any clause that exempts the engineer or contractor from the 10-year guarantee liability is automatically null and void. The law cannot be contracted out of. The contractor and engineer remain fully liable for 10 years regardless of what the contract says.

MediumArticles 657 vs 658
Compare unit price contracts (Art. 657) and lump sum contracts (Art. 658) regarding cost increases. What is the key difference?
▾

Unit Price (Art. 657): If actual costs exceed the estimate, the contractor must notify the employer of the required increase (variation order). If he doesn't notify, his right to the extra payment is forfeited. If the increase is too large, the employer may suspend work and pay only for what's been done.

Lump Sum (Art. 658): The contractor cannot request extra money for design adjustments UNLESS: (a) the employer agrees to the increase, or (b) the increase results from the employer's mistake. Price hikes due to inflation do not count — only war or natural disasters justify compensation.

Key difference: Unit price = contractor can request increases (but must notify). Lump sum = contractor generally absorbs all cost changes.

MediumArticle 650
A contractor performs defective work on a building. The defect cannot be fixed. What happens under Article 650?
▾

If the defect is impossible to fix, the contract is terminated without warning — the contractor receives no notice period. The contract simply ends.

Compare with fixable defects: if the defect can be repaired, the contractor is first given a chance to repair. If they fail to do so, the employer can either rescind the contract OR hire another contractor to repair it at the original contractor's expense.

MediumArticle 661
When is a contractor NOT allowed to assign a subcontractor under Article 661?
▾

Two exceptions where subcontracting is prohibited:

1. Contract specifies personal performance — the contract explicitly states the contractor must do the work themselves.
2. Work requires the original contractor's specific competence — the nature of the work inherently requires the contractor's particular expertise that a subcontractor wouldn't have.

In both cases, the original contractor cannot delegate the work. If they do so anyway, they remain fully responsible for the subcontractor's actions.

HardArticle 666-667
A contractor who has been personally building a custom mosque dies halfway through construction. His heirs want to continue. What determines whether they can under Article 666? What must the employer do under Article 667?
▾

Under Article 666 — can the contract continue?
It depends on whether the contractor's personal qualifications were essential. For a custom mosque with specific craftsmanship, the contractor's personal skills are likely essential and the heirs presumably don't have the same qualifications → the contract expires.

If the work were generic (e.g., standard concrete work), personal qualifications may not be essential → contract could continue.

Under Article 667 — what must the employer do?
Since the contract expires:
1. Pay the heirs for the value of work already completed.
2. Request the drawings and prepared materials and pay compensation for them.
3. The same rules apply if the contractor becomes unable to perform for any unavoidable reason (e.g., serious illness).

HardArticle 665
A sudden fire (not caused by either party) destroys 40% of completed work and all materials. Who bears the losses under Article 665?
▾

Under Article 665 — sudden accident with no party at fault:

1. Employer does NOT pay the contractor for the destroyed completed work (contractor bears that loss).
2. Material loss falls on whoever supplied the materials — if the employer supplied them, employer bears the loss; if the contractor supplied them, contractor bears the loss.

Compare with fault scenarios:
• Contractor at fault → contractor compensates employer for all employer-supplied material.
• Employer at fault → employer bears all material losses AND must compensate the contractor for damages too.

5.1 — Critical Thinking Definition

Critical thinking is routinely applying intellectual standards to the elements of thought as they seek to develop the traits of a mature engineering mind.

5.2 — 10 Intellectual Standards

  1. Clarity — understandable; meaning can be grasped
  2. Accuracy — free from errors or distortions; true
  3. Relevance — relating to the matter at hand
  4. Logicalness — parts make sense together; no contradictions
  5. Breadth — encompassing multiple viewpoints (wide)
  1. Precision — exact to the necessary level of detail
  2. Significance — focusing on the important, not trivial
  3. Completeness — something is whole; nothing is missing
  4. Fairness — justifiable; non-self-serving; not one-sided
  5. Depth — containing complexities and multiple viewpoints (deep)
Breadth = encompassing multiple viewpoints (wide/broad). Depth = complexities (deep). Don't mix them up!

5.3 — 8 Elements of Thought

  • Purposes — whenever we think, we think for a purpose
  • Questions — to answer a question or solve a problem
  • Points of view — within a point of view
  • Assumptions — based on assumptions
  • Implications — leading to implications and consequences
  • Information — we use data, facts, and experiences
  • Inferences — to make inferences and judgments
  • Concepts — based on concepts and theories

5.4 — 8 Intellectual Traits

Humility

Knowledge of ignorance — sensitive to what you know and what you DON'T know.

Autonomy

Thinking for oneself while adhering to standards of rationality.

Integrity

Holding yourself to the same standards you expect from others — NO DOUBLE STANDARDS.

Courage

The disposition to question beliefs about which you feel strongly.

Perseverance

Working through intellectual complexities despite frustrations inherent in the task.

Confidence in Reason

Belief that human interests are best served by giving freest play to reason.

Empathy

Awareness of the need to actively entertain views that differ from your own — especially those you strongly disagree with.

Fairmindedness

Treating all viewpoints alike without reference to one's own feelings or vested interests.

5.5 — Deduction vs. Induction

Deductive Reasoning

Reasoning from general premises to specific, certain conclusions. "Formal logic."

Conclusions are: certain · inevitable · inescapable
Major premise: All tortoises are vegetarians ``` Minor premise: Bessie is a tortoise Conclusion: Therefore Bessie IS a vegetarian
Inductive Reasoning

Reasoning from specific cases to general, uncertain conclusions. “Informal / everyday logic.”

Conclusions are: probable · reasonable · plausible · believable
Observation: Biff has an anchor tattoo on his arm Conclusion: He PROBABLY served in the Navy
Neither form is preferred over the other — both are equally compelling and persuasive (Hollihan & Baske, 1994).

EasyTraits
Define Intellectual Integrity. What makes it different from Intellectual Humility?

MediumStandards Trap
What is the difference between "Breadth" and "Depth" as intellectual standards? Students frequently confuse them.
▾

Breadth: Encompassing multiple viewpoints — thinking widely across different perspectives. Wide coverage. Example: considering an engineering decision from the perspectives of the client, the public, regulators, and workers.

Depth: Containing complexities — thinking deeply into the nuances of a single question. Thorough exploration. Example: fully understanding all the physical mechanisms behind a structural failure rather than just knowing it collapsed.

Memory aid: Breadth = WIDE. Depth = DEEP.

MediumReasoning
Classify each: (a) "All engineers must be licensed → Ahmed is an engineer → Ahmed must be licensed." (b) "Every bridge I've inspected this year had corrosion → bridges in this region probably have a corrosion problem."
▾

(a) Deductive reasoning. Starts with a known general rule (all engineers must be licensed) and applies it to a specific case (Ahmed) to reach a certain conclusion. Classic syllogism structure.

(b) Inductive reasoning. Starts with specific observations (each individual bridge) and draws a general but uncertain conclusion about all bridges in the region. The conclusion is probable but could be wrong — there may be bridges without corrosion that weren't inspected.

HardElements of Thought
Apply the 8 elements of thought to the following engineering decision: "Should we proceed with construction despite the subsoil report suggesting potential instability?"
▾

Purpose: Make a safe, cost-effective decision about whether to proceed with construction.
Question: Is the subsoil sufficiently stable to proceed, and what are the risks if we don't investigate further?
Point of view: Engineering safety perspective, client's financial perspective, public safety perspective.
Assumptions: That the subsoil report is accurate; that known engineering standards apply; that budget constraints are fixed.
Information: The subsoil report, site geology data, structural load calculations, similar case precedents.
Inferences: If instability is confirmed, foundation design must change; if ignored, collapse risk increases.
Concepts: Geotechnical engineering, factor of safety, building codes, negligence.
Implications: Proceeding without investigation could lead to structural failure, liability under Article 651, and loss of life.

HardRoofline Example
Discuss the power generation table: Companies 1 (3450 MW gen, 3000 MW load, $100/MWh), 2 (1488 MW, 1200 MW, $50/MWh), 3 (6220 MW, 6900 MW, $25/MWh). Which company needs extra power and what is the cheapest strategy?
▾

Reserve Power = Generated Capacity − Load Supplied:
Company 1: 3450 − 3000 = +450 MW reserve
Company 2: 1488 − 1200 = +288 MW reserve
Company 3: 6220 − 6900 = −680 MW DEFICIT

Company 3 needs 680 MW of extra power.

Cheapest strategy (use inductive reasoning on costs):
Company 2 is cheaper ($50/MWh vs $100/MWh for Company 1).
Buy all 288 MW from Company 2 first → 680 − 288 = 392 MW still needed.
Buy remaining 392 MW from Company 1 at $100/MWh.

Additional observations: Company 3 has the largest average unit capacity (6220÷18 = 345.6 MW/unit). Company 2 has the smallest (1488÷17 = 87.5 MW/unit).
