Listening Skills & Effective Studying

ASU 113 — Dr. Mohamed Mokhtar

👂 How to Be a Good Listener
  1. Review your notes before class
  2. Arrive early — go to class a few minutes before it starts
  3. Maintain natural eye contact with the speaker
  4. Show alertness through your posture
  5. Ignore distractions — side talks, external noise
  6. Take notes on the speaker's most important points and prepare questions
  7. Turn off cell phones
📝 Cornell Note-Taking Method

A structured note-taking system using three sections on the page:

  • Cue Column (left, 2.5" wide) — keywords, questions, cues written after class
  • Note-Taking Area (right, 6" wide) — lecture notes during class
  • Summaries (bottom, 2" tall) — brief summary of the page's content

The 5 R's of Cornell: Record → Reduce → Recite → Reflect → Review

📚 Guidelines for Effective Studying
  1. Connect class work to long-range goals
  2. Become active & involved in learning
  3. Time scheduling — make a weekly master plan and a semester calendar
  4. Stick to your schedule — fight laziness, daydreaming; practice patience
  5. Study difficult classes early afternoon
  6. Begin test prep a week in advance
  7. Incorporate self-testing into your study schedule
  8. Best time to study is right after class
  9. Follow a good study strategy — SQ3R
  10. Practice a good test-taking strategy
⏰ Why Scheduling?
  • Reduces wasted time — saves time overall
  • Separates work and play — promotes life balance
  • Helps prevent cramming

👂 Being a Good Listener — The 7 Steps ▶

The lecture frames listening as an active skill, not passive hearing. The goal is to leave every talk as a "more informed person." The seven steps form a before → during → always cycle:

  • Before class: Review previous notes (step 1), arrive early (step 2), turn off cell phones (step 7)
  • During class: Maintain eye contact (step 3), alert posture (step 4), ignore distractions (step 5), take notes & prepare questions (step 6)

Exam trap: Questions may test whether you can distinguish between before-class prep (reviewing notes, arriving early) and during-class behavior (eye contact, posture, note-taking).

📝 Cornell Note-Taking — Structure & The 5 R's ▶

The Cornell method divides a standard 8.5" × 11" page into three zones:

  • Cue Column (left, 2.5"): After class — write keywords, questions, prompts that connect to the notes on the right
  • Note-Taking Area (right, 6"): During class — capture main ideas, details, diagrams
  • Summary Section (bottom, 2"): After class — condense the page into 1–2 sentence summary

The 5 R's guide how you use the system: Record (capture notes during lecture) → Reduce (distill into cue column) → Recite (cover notes, use cues to recall) → Reflect (think about connections) → Review (revisit regularly).

Exam trap: The dimensions (2.5", 6", 2") and the names of the 5 R's are direct-recall targets.

📉 The Forgetting Curve — Key Numbers ▶

The forgetting curve (from the lecture's chart) compares two groups:

  • No same-day review: ~100% on same day → ~40% after one day → ~20% after two weeks → even lower by nine weeks
  • Same-day review: ~100% → ~85% after one day → ~80% at two weeks → ~65% at nine weeks

The takeaway: reviewing material the same day you learn it dramatically flattens the forgetting curve. This directly supports guideline #8 (best time to study is right after class).

Exam trap: The forgetting curve is the scientific rationale behind "study right after class" — expect a question linking these two concepts together.

🔍 SQ3R Method — Full Breakdown ▶

SQ3R is an active reading technique attributed to George Wilson in the lecture:

  • Survey: Skim the chapter — headings, subheadings, bold terms, images, summaries
  • Question: Convert headings into questions to guide your reading
  • Read: Read actively, seeking answers to your questions
  • Recite: After each section, answer your questions aloud or in writing without looking
  • Review: Go back over the whole chapter, check your answers, reinforce weak areas

Exam trap: Know the exact order (Survey comes first, not Read). Also know that SQ3R is specifically a reading/studying strategy, not a test-taking strategy.

✅ Test-Taking Tips — The 7 Tips ▶
  • Arrive on time — being late increases anxiety and wastes exam time
  • Do a memory dump — before reading any questions, write down key facts/formulas you memorized
  • Read directions carefully — misunderstanding instructions costs easy marks
  • Budget time wisely — allocate time per section, don't get stuck on one question
  • Practice relaxation — deep breathing, staying calm under pressure
  • Answer ALL questions — never leave blanks (especially in MCQ where there's no penalty)
  • Use ALL the time — don't leave early; review your answers

Exam trap: "Memory dump" is a specific test-taking tip, not a study strategy. Don't confuse test-taking tips with study guidelines.
